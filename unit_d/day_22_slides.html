Why this matters

You've been staring at your own screens for days. You know where everything is, so everything "makes sense" to you.

Your users don't have that context. A 10-minute test will reveal more than another hour of self-critique.

Get your work in front of real eyes — classmates first, then outside users for D9.

Figma Prototype Setup

  • Starting frame — Set the entry point for your flow
  • Hotspot connections — Link screens with click/tap targets
  • Share settings — "Anyone with the link" for remote testing
  • Present full-screen — Hide Figma UI so it feels like a real app

Show of hands — who has at least 5 screens connected? Who needs to set up hotspots today?

What changes when it looks real

Users stop saying "I get the idea" and start noticing details

Watch for: hesitations, misreads of hierarchy, tapping the wrong element, confused expressions they don't voice

Your question shifts: not "Does this concept make sense?" but "Can you complete this task without help?"

In-Class
Peer Testing

Pair up with someone you haven't worked with recently

Testing Rounds

Round 1 (15 min) — Person A tests Person B's prototype

Give 2-3 realistic tasks. Observer: watch silently, note hesitations. Debrief: top 3 observations.

Round 2 (15 min) — Switch roles

Wrap-up (10 min) — Write down individually:

3 specific issues found. For each: what you'll change and why. This becomes the start of your D9 documentation.

Next up

Work session (110 min) — Fix issues from peer testing first. Continue building high-fidelity screens. Experiment with Smart Animate. Set up your prototype link for outside testing.

Day 21 checklist — use it as you work:

  1. Hierarchy — 3 levels visible in 3 seconds?
  2. Spacing — 8pt grid, groups clearly separated?
  3. Typography — 4 sizes max, correct letter spacing?
  4. Color — Accent at ~10%, text passes 4.5:1?
  5. Alignment — Left edges align, concentric radii?
  6. Restraint — Can you remove anything without losing info?
